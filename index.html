Voyages

Why so many people use world regions to reach their target.

Introduction

While playing our first few Wikispeedia games [play], we quickly noticed that we tend to adopt a strategy looking for articles about countries and world regions to reach the goal, sometimes regardless of the article we start in or need to reach. This made us wonder whether our strategy - which we call Wikispeedia Voyages - is a good strategy, or at least popular with other Wikispeedia players too.

The goal in the Wikispeedia game is to reach a target Wikipedia article from a start article with as little steps and as quickly as possible, just by clicking on links to the next page. The dataset [link] collects both information about players' behaviour and the network structure of Wikipedia articles, allowing to analyse Voyages under multiple perspectives. Our aim is to understand to what extent the network structure plays a role and whether users genuinely fancy Voyages.

Research Questions

  1. Does the Wikispeedia article network structure intrinsically favour Wikispeedia Voyages?
  2. Are users more comfortable, fast or efficient with world regions than other topics?
  3. How does the strategy compare with the algorithmic shortest paths?

Markov

Explain KL and Markov Suppose we have two 2D matrices: \( P \) representing transition probabilities for users, and \( Q \) representing transition probabilities for a Markov chain. Each entry \( P_{ij} \) and \( Q_{ij} \) corresponds to the transition probability from state \( i \) to state \( j \).

The KL divergence is computed element-wise as \( D_{KL}(P || Q)_{ij} = P_{ij} \log \frac{P_{ij}}{Q_{ij}} \) only for valid entries where \( P_{ij} > 0 \) and \( Q_{ij} > 0 \) and is set to \( 0 \) otherwise.

Next, for each column \( j \), the mean KL divergence over all rows \( i \) is calculated as:

\[ \overline{D_{KL}}(j) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} D_{KL}(\mathbf{P} || \mathbf{Q})_{ij} \]

Here, \( N \) is the number of rows (states). The result is a vector \( \overline{D_{KL}} \), where each element corresponds to the mean divergence for a particular column.

Let's start with a simple example just to understand how we compare the user paths to random walks in the network. We call the user transitions matrix \(P\), the Markov chain transitions \(Q\) and the resulting element-wise KL divergence \(D_{KL}\)

\( P = \begin{bmatrix} 0.2 & 0.8 \\ 0.6 & 0.4 \end{bmatrix} \quad \text{and} \quad Q = \begin{bmatrix} 0.1 & 0.9 \\ 0.5 & 0.5 \end{bmatrix} \quad \text{give} \quad D_{KL} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.1386 & -0.0942 \\ 0.4159 & -0.2238 \end{bmatrix} \)

We see that when users make a certain transition more than expected by the random walk, it results in a positive \(KL\) divergence. Similarly, making a transition less than expected by the random walk results in a negative \(KL\) divergence, and having the same probability as the random walk results in \(0\) divergence. Generally, if users transition to a certain article more than random, the column corresponding to that article will have a lot of positive values. If we find columns where the sum of \(KL\) divergences is large, then users tend to choose these articles more than the randomness that may favour these articles in the first place!

In our example for instance, the random network transitions rather favour transitions to the second article, but despite this users transition more often to the first article. The \(KL\) summed by columns gives \([0.5545, -0.3180]\), where the choices of the users to choose an article over the random transitions is clearly displayed.
